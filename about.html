About

Scholar-practitioner, by design.

Ten-plus years teaching adults, leading programs, and building the systems that keep institutional knowledge from walking out the door — now anchored in doctoral research on the theory–practice gap.

01 — The path

How I got here

I started teaching before I knew that was what I was doing. As a Human Resources Specialist with the 126th Infantry Regiment, I was responsible for making sure battalion personnel understood things that mattered — benefits, promotions, mandatory training, harassment and diversity curricula. The audience was adults with competing demands, varying literacy with the material, and no patience for a lecture that wasted their time. That is where I learned that instruction either changes what someone can do, or it is decoration.

At Mears Group I moved from supporting technology to leading it. As an IT Support Specialist I spent five years translating complex systems for non-technical staff one conversation at a time, and writing the troubleshooting guides and standard operating procedures that let the next technician skip the learning curve entirely. Promoted twice in four years, I ended up as IT Project Administrator running cross-functional delivery for a regional operations environment — 15+ professionals, 5+ major releases, budgets from $150,000 to $500,000, and more than 12,000 combined resource hours, all delivered on time and within budget. The training and adoption programs I designed around those rollouts were what made the deployments stick.

Since 2022 I have supported the Department of Veterans Affairs' Electronic Health Record Modernization program through Take2 Consulting — the transition of more than 170 medical facilities from the legacy VistA system to a unified Federal EHR. My work there is analytical and consultative: executive dashboards that make operational reality legible to leadership, root cause analysis on the failures that keep recurring, and the knowledge-transfer artifacts that preserve continuity across contract and team transitions.

The doctorate pulled all of it into focus. My EdD research is a classical grounded theory study of how students and faculty in community college Health Sciences programs navigate the balance between classroom instruction and clinical practice. It is, in essence, an academic account of the problem I have spent my whole career working on from the operational side.

02 — Leadership

Leadership philosophy

Programs rarely fail in the curriculum, and they rarely fail in the people. They fail in the seam — the space between what a program says it delivers and what its graduates can actually do on the first day of practice. My research named that space. Most of my career has been spent working inside it.

The goal is not a program with no gap. The goal is a program that knows where its gap is, and has a deliberate practice for managing it.

Name the gap honestly

The most damaging thing a program can do is insist the seam is not there. In my study, both students and faculty identified the same main concern long before anyone had language for it. Leaders who invite that conversation get usable data; leaders who treat it as a criticism get silence and a slow drift toward irrelevance. I would rather run a program that can articulate its own weak points than one that reports clean numbers it does not believe.

Design for the role, not the average

One of the clearest findings in my research was that role position determines what a person can actually do about a problem. Students responded to the gap by building self-directed study systems; faculty responded by adjusting pedagogy. Same concern, entirely different levers. Institutional interventions that ignore this — a single initiative aimed at everyone — reliably underperform. Good program leadership asks who holds which lever, and resources accordingly.

Make the improvement durable

I have led enough transitions to know that the real risk is not the change itself but what evaporates afterward. Documentation, methodology artifacts, standardized workflows, and honest handoffs are not administrative overhead — they are how an institution keeps what it learned. I build them into the work from the start, on the assumption that I will not be the one running the program forever.

Lead with evidence, decide with judgment

I spend a great deal of time making data legible: dashboards, KPIs, root cause analysis, outcome mapping. That work earns its keep only when it sharpens a decision somebody has to make. Metrics are an input to judgment, not a substitute for it, and a leader who cannot explain a number's limitations should not be acting on it.

03 — Teaching

Teaching and learning philosophy

I have taught soldiers, field technicians, project managers, and executives. What they had in common is what every adult learner has in common: existing expertise, limited time, and an immediate practical reason for being in the room. Andragogy is not a theoretical preference for me — it is the only thing that has ever worked with the people I teach.

Start from what the learner already carries

Adults arrive with a working model of the domain, and that model is the most valuable instructional asset available. My job is to find where it is accurate and build on it, and where it is misaligned and surface that productively. Instruction that ignores prior experience wastes the learner's strongest resource and quietly insults them.

Anchor every objective to a performance

I write learning outcomes as things a person can be observed doing, then map assessment directly to them. If I cannot describe what competent performance looks like, the objective is not ready. This is also the discipline that keeps the theory–practice gap from widening: outcomes tied to real practice are harder to drift away from.

Choose the modality the content deserves

Blended delivery is not a compromise between classroom and online — it is a design decision about which parts of the content need presence and which do not. Procedural knowledge often travels well asynchronously. Judgment, escalation, and anything with social stakes generally does not. I have designed both, and I have traveled to sites to run hands-on instruction when that was what the material actually required.

Build the artifacts that outlive the session

Quick-reference guides, knowledge base articles, standard operating procedures, facilitator notes. Training that exists only in the room ends when the room empties. The reusable support library is often the more valuable deliverable, and it is the one that accelerates every person who joins after you.

Treat assessment as feedback, not verdict

The point of learner assessment is to tell both of us what to do next. When results are disappointing, the first question is what the design got wrong — sequencing, practice opportunity, unclear expectations — before it is a question about the learner.
