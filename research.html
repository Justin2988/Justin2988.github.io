Doctoral research · EdD, University of Phoenix

Instructional Balance in Community College Health Sciences Programs A Qualitative Classical Grounded Theory Study

Doctoral candidate (ABD) · Expected 2026

01 — Abstract

Abstract

Community college Health Sciences programs in the United States prepare learners for professional entry through a combination of theoretical and practical instruction. While variations in instructional balance have been documented, the underlying social processes stakeholders use to navigate these environments remained largely unexplained prior to this study. The purpose of this qualitative, classical grounded theory study was to discover the process through which students and faculty engaged with instructional balance in community college Health Sciences programs across the Great Lakes region. Data were collected via anonymous, open-ended electronic surveys from an analytic sample of n = 18 participants, including 12 students and 6 faculty members. Constant comparative analysis revealed that participants' main concern was the gap between classroom instruction and clinical or professional practice. The emergent core category, bridging the taught-and-lived gap, described the Basic Social Process unfolding across five stages, namely Encountering, Diagnosing, Mobilizing, Enacting, and Consolidating. The study identified that role position determines the mechanisms for accessible enactment, with students focusing on self-directed study systems and faculty implementing pedagogical adjustments. These findings implied that institutional leaders should reframe program goals to focus on managing rather than eliminating the recurring theory-practice gap.

03 — Key finding

Role position determines the available lever

Students and faculty shared the same main concern — but the mechanisms accessible to each for acting on it were entirely different.

Students

Enacted the process primarily through self-directed study systems — individual strategies built to compensate for the distance between what was taught and what practice demanded.

Faculty

Enacted the process primarily through pedagogical adjustments — changes to instruction itself, made within the constraints of the program as designed.

Institutional leaders should reframe program goals to focus on managing — rather than eliminating — the recurring theory-practice gap.

That implication is the reason this research matters to administration and not only to instruction. A program that treats the gap as a defect to be engineered away will keep rediscovering it. A program that treats it as a recurring condition can build a deliberate practice around it: naming it, resourcing the different levers each role actually holds, and consolidating what works so the next cohort does not start over.

04 — Agenda

Research interests

Where I expect to keep working, in scholarship and in practice.

Theory–practice integration

How professional and workforce education programs manage the distance between classroom instruction and the demands of first-day practice.

Community college & workforce education

Program design, student success, and institutional decision-making in two-year and professional-entry settings.

Adult learning & andragogy

How adult professional learners actually acquire and consolidate competence, and what instructional design owes them.

Grounded theory methodology

Classical grounded theory as a tool for explaining process in education settings where the operative concern has not yet been named.

Program assessment & evaluation

Building outcome and competency measures that inform institutional decisions rather than merely satisfying reporting requirements.

Faculty development

Supporting the pedagogical adjustments faculty are already making, and making that work visible at the program level.
