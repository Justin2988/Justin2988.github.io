Program Leadership Take2 Consulting · U.S. Dept. of Veterans Affairs

Electronic Health Record Modernization

Analytical and consulting support for the enterprise transition of more than 170 VA medical facilities from the legacy VistA system to a unified Federal EHR.
Role

Support Analyst · Consultant, VA OIT Support — IT Operations

Organization

Take2 Consulting, LLC

Period

January 2022 — Present

Scope

170+ medical facilities, enterprise-wide

01 Context

The Department of Veterans Affairs is replacing VistA — a system that has run the country's largest integrated health network for decades — with a unified Federal electronic health record built on Oracle Health. The transition spans more than 170 medical facilities, each with its own workflows, staffing realities, and clinical dependencies.

A program at that scale generates enormous volumes of operational signal: deployment status, incidents, events, dependencies, escalations. The difficulty is not collecting it. The difficulty is that the people who have to make decisions about a facility's readiness cannot see the picture in time to act on it, and the knowledge required to interpret it lives in the heads of whoever happens to be on the contract that year.

02 My role

I provide analytical and consulting support for large-scale public-sector IT operations across the EHRM program — improving project tracking, issue management, and reporting for the enterprise transition. In practice that means sitting between operational data and the cross-functional stakeholders who need to act on it, and making sure the distance between those two things stays short.

03 Approach

  • Executive reporting infrastructure. Designed and maintain executive-level Power BI dashboards — including the Deployment Support Operations (DSO) and Event dashboards — built with Power Query and DAX, translating complex OIT operational data into clear, actionable insight for program leadership.
  • Systemic root cause analysis. Conduct root cause analyses on recurring enterprise IT operations issues, synthesizing data from multiple sources to identify systemic patterns rather than treating each recurrence as a new incident.
  • Knowledge-base development. Document solutions into the knowledge base and implement preventive measures, so a resolved problem becomes an organizational asset instead of a one-time fix.
  • Automation and acceleration. Leverage generative AI, prompt engineering, and Power Platform low-code automation to accelerate documentation, prototype reporting workflows, and reduce manual effort on routine analytical tasks.
  • Transition and knowledge transfer. Lead knowledge-transfer activities, authoring handoff documentation, methodology artifacts, and standardized reporting workflows that preserve institutional knowledge across contract and team transitions.

04 Outcome

  • Improved project tracking, issue management, and reporting across the enterprise transition.
  • Complex operational data made legible and actionable for cross-functional stakeholders and program leadership.
  • Documented knowledge-base solutions and preventive measures that reduce service disruptions for clinical staff and, ultimately, for veterans.
  • Institutional knowledge and operational continuity preserved across contract and team transitions.

The reporting is not the deliverable. A decision somebody can defend is the deliverable.

05 Why this matters in higher education

Institutions run the same problem under different names. A multi-campus system standardizing a student information system, an academic unit preparing for accreditation review, a provost's office trying to see program health across departments — all of it is the work of making distributed operational reality legible to the person who has to decide, and then keeping the resulting knowledge from evaporating when the people change.

The competencies transfer directly: enterprise change management, stakeholder reporting, evidence-based analysis, and knowledge management under conditions where failure has consequences for people who did not choose to be affected by it.

Assessment & Evaluation Take2 Consulting · U.S. Dept. of Veterans Affairs

Executive Decision-Support Dashboards

Power BI reporting built with Power Query and DAX that turned distributed operational data into a single actionable view for program leadership.
Role

Support Analyst · Consultant, VA OIT Support

Organization

Take2 Consulting, LLC

Period

January 2022 — Present

Stack

Power BI · Power Query · DAX · Power Platform

01 Context

Enterprise IT operations for a program spanning 170+ facilities produce operational data faster than any leadership team can absorb it, and from more sources than any one person tracks. Deployment status lives in one place, incidents in another, events in a third. Each is accurate. None of them, alone, answers the question a decision-maker is actually asking.

The result is a familiar failure mode: reporting that is technically complete and practically useless. Leadership either waits for a human to assemble the picture manually — which is slow and does not scale — or makes the call on partial information.

02 My role

I design and maintain the executive-level Power BI dashboards for the program, including the Deployment Support Operations (DSO) and Event dashboards. The work spans the full path: understanding what decision a stakeholder is trying to make, modeling the data to support it, and building the visualization that makes the answer obvious.

03 Approach

  • Start from the decision, not the data. A dashboard earns its place only if someone changes what they do because of it. Every measure traces back to a question a stakeholder has to answer.
  • Model before you visualize. Built the transformation and modeling layer in Power Query and DAX so that the data arriving at the visual is already trustworthy — most dashboard failures are modeling failures wearing a chart.
  • Design for a mixed audience. The same dashboard serves program leadership and the cross-functional teams doing the work. Those audiences need different altitudes of the same truth, without maintaining two versions of it.
  • Connect reporting to root cause. Paired the dashboards with root cause analysis on recurring issues, so the reporting surfaced systemic patterns instead of just counting incidents.
  • Automate the routine. Applied Power Platform low-code automation and generative AI tooling to prototype reporting workflows and cut manual effort, improving turnaround on stakeholder deliverables.

04 Outcome

  • Complex OIT operational data translated into clear, actionable insight for cross-functional stakeholders and program leadership.
  • Systemic patterns identified across recurring issues, enabling preventive measures rather than repeated remediation.
  • Faster turnaround and higher quality on stakeholder deliverables through automation of routine analytical work.
  • Reporting workflows standardized and documented, so the capability survived team transitions.

05 Why this matters in higher education

This is the core work of institutional research and academic assessment. Enrollment trends, retention and completion, program-level learning outcomes, accreditation evidence, resource utilization — all of it is distributed across systems that were never designed to talk to each other, and all of it has to reach a dean, provost, or board in a form that supports a decision.

My research reinforces the caution I bring to it: metrics are an input to judgment, not a replacement for it. An assessment regime that produces clean numbers nobody believes has failed, no matter how complete the dataset.

The measure of a reporting system is not how much it shows. It is how often it changes what someone decides.

Instructional Design Mears Group

Enterprise Training & Adoption Program

End-user training and onboarding for multi-site software rollouts, designed on adult-learning principles to build competency and reduce post-deployment support demand.
Role

IT Project Administrator

Organization

Mears Group, Inc.

Period

August 2021 — February 2022

Scale

15+ IT professionals · 5+ major releases · Multi-site

01 Context

A regional operations technology environment was moving through a dense sequence of software releases — five or more major rollouts, with budgets between $150,000 and $500,000 and more than 12,000 combined resource hours. The technology side of a deployment is bounded and knowable. The human side is not.

Field staff across multiple sites were being asked to change how they did their jobs, often mid-season, often with no appetite for it. Deployments that ignore this produce a predictable signature: a spike in support tickets, workarounds that quietly defeat the system's purpose, and adoption numbers that look fine on paper while the old process survives underground.

02 My role

I led cross-functional IT project delivery for the environment, managing 15+ IT professionals across the release portfolio. Within that, I owned the design and delivery of the end-user training and onboarding programs that accompanied each rollout — treating enablement as part of the deployment rather than something bolted on after go-live.

03 Approach

  • Design from andragogy, not from the feature list. Training was built around what staff needed to be able to do, applying adult-learning and instructional-design principles rather than walking through software menus.
  • Layered materials for different moments of need. Full training programs for initial competency, quick-reference guides for the moment of use, and knowledge-base documentation for the question that arises three weeks later.
  • Hands-on facilitation at the site. Traveled onsite to deliver instruction in person during product rollouts, where the material required presence and where resistance is best addressed face to face.
  • Standardization across teams. Built documentation that made learning consistent from site to site, so a technician moving between locations encountered the same expectations and the same reference material.
  • Onboarding as a reusable asset. The same library that supported the rollout accelerated onboarding for new staff afterward — the training outlasted the project that created it.

04 Outcome

  • Delivered every initiative across the release portfolio on time and within budget — 12,000+ combined resource hours.
  • Built staff competency and drove technology adoption across sites.
  • Reduced post-deployment support demand — the clearest available signal that the instruction worked.
  • Standardized learning across teams and measurably accelerated onboarding.
  • Earned two promotions in four years in recognition of performance, training leadership, and cross-functional collaboration.

Reduced support demand after a rollout is the honest measure of training. It is the one number that cannot be produced by a well-attended session nobody learned anything in.

05 Why this matters in higher education

This is faculty and staff development, with the vocabulary changed. A new LMS, a revised assessment platform, a change to advising workflow — each asks experienced professionals to alter established practice, and each fails the same way when enablement is treated as an afterthought.

The design discipline is identical: define the performance, build to it, meet learners where the work happens, and leave behind artifacts that keep working after the project team disbands.

Instructional Design Michigan National Guard

Battalion Blended Learning Program

Classroom instruction combined with digital training for battalion personnel, including organizational development and mandatory diversity and harassment-awareness curricula.
Role

Human Resources Specialist · Team Leader

Organization

Michigan National Guard — 126th Infantry Regiment

Period

October 2012 — October 2018

Reach

Battalion level · 150+ personnel supported

01 Context

Battalion personnel are a demanding instructional audience in the most useful way. They are adults with real expertise, competing obligations outside the unit, wide variation in prior exposure to the material, and drill time that is finite and contested. Nobody is there to be talked at.

The subject matter ranged from administrative processes people needed in order to be paid and promoted correctly, to organizational development and mandatory curricula on diversity and harassment awareness — content where a session that produces compliance without comprehension has accomplished nothing.

02 My role

As a Human Resources Specialist I designed and delivered the learning solutions supporting battalion personnel, and was promoted to team leader overseeing 5+ soldiers. Alongside instruction I managed the full HR lifecycle — onboarding, benefits, payroll, promotions, retirements, and separations — for 150+ personnel.

03 Approach

  • Blend by content type, not by convenience. Combined classroom instruction with digital training, assigning each mode the material it actually suited — procedural knowledge asynchronously, judgment and socially weighted content in the room.
  • Design toward required outcomes. Applied instructional design and adult-learning principles to build the knowledge, skills, and behavioral outcomes the unit's objectives required — starting from the performance, not the slide deck.
  • Facilitate sensitive curricula seriously. Delivered diversity and harassment-awareness training at battalion level, where facilitation quality determines whether the session changes anything or hardens resistance.
  • Treat service delivery as instruction too. Ran the HR lifecycle with an emphasis on responsive customer service, which in practice meant explaining systems clearly enough that people could navigate them independently next time.

04 Outcome

  • Blended learning solutions delivered across battalion personnel, building the knowledge, skills, and behavioral outcomes required for unit objectives.
  • Organizational development and mandatory training programs facilitated at battalion level.
  • Promotion to team leader over 5+ soldiers.
  • Full HR lifecycle managed for 150+ personnel.

An audience that will tell you immediately when you are wasting their time is the best instructional design training available.

05 Why this matters in higher education

Adult and non-traditional learners are the majority population at many institutions, and they behave exactly like the audience described above: experienced, time-poor, instrumentally motivated, and quick to disengage from instruction that does not respect those conditions.

The facilitation experience carries over as directly as the design experience. Required training on difficult subjects is a permanent feature of institutional life, and the difference between a session that lands and one that breeds cynicism is almost entirely in how it is designed and led.

04 — Research

Doctoral research

EdD, University of Phoenix · Doctoral candidate (ABD) · Expected 2026

Instructional Balance in Community College Health Sciences Programs A Qualitative Classical Grounded Theory Study

Community college Health Sciences programs prepare learners for professional entry through a combination of theoretical and practical instruction. Variations in that balance have been documented, but the social processes through which students and faculty navigate it had not been explained.

Constant comparative analysis of anonymous, open-ended survey responses from 18 students and faculty members across the Great Lakes region surfaced a shared main concern: the gap between classroom instruction and clinical practice.

Design

Qualitative classical grounded theory

Data

Anonymous, open-ended electronic surveys

Sample

n = 18 · 12 students, 6 faculty · Great Lakes region

Analysis

Constant comparative method

Read the formal abstract

Community college Health Sciences programs in the United States prepare learners for professional entry through a combination of theoretical and practical instruction. While variations in instructional balance have been documented, the underlying social processes stakeholders use to navigate these environments remained largely unexplained prior to this study. The purpose of this qualitative, classical grounded theory study was to discover the process through which students and faculty engaged with instructional balance in community college Health Sciences programs across the Great Lakes region. Data were collected via anonymous, open-ended electronic surveys from an analytic sample of n = 18 participants, including 12 students and 6 faculty members. Constant comparative analysis revealed that participants' main concern was the gap between classroom instruction and clinical or professional practice. The emergent core category, bridging the taught-and-lived gap, described the Basic Social Process unfolding across five stages, namely Encountering, Diagnosing, Mobilizing, Enacting, and Consolidating. The study identified that role position determines the mechanisms accessible for enactment, with students focusing on self-directed study systems and faculty implementing pedagogical adjustments. These findings implied that institutional leaders should reframe program goals to focus on managing rather than eliminating the recurring theory–practice gap.

Key finding: role position determines the available lever

Students and faculty shared the same main concern — but the mechanisms accessible to each for acting on it were entirely different.

Students

Enacted the process primarily through self-directed study systems — individual strategies built to compensate for the distance between what was taught and what practice demanded.

Faculty

Enacted the process primarily through pedagogical adjustments — changes to instruction itself, made within the constraints of the program as designed.

Institutional leaders should reframe program goals to focus on managing, rather than eliminating, the recurring theory–practice gap.

That implication is the reason this research matters to administration and not only to instruction. A program that treats the gap as a defect to be engineered away will keep rediscovering it. A program that treats it as a recurring condition can build a deliberate practice around it: naming it, resourcing the different levers each role actually holds, and consolidating what works so the next cohort does not start over.

05 — Philosophy

How I lead, and how I teach

Two full statements — the kind a search committee asks for — each shaped by the same research finding: the goal is not a program with no gap, but a program that knows where its gap is and manages it deliberately.

Leadership philosophy

Programs rarely fail in the curriculum, and they rarely fail in the people. They fail in the seam — the space between what a program says it delivers and what its graduates can actually do on the first day of practice. My research named that space. Most of my career has been spent working inside it.

The goal is not a program with no gap. The goal is a program that knows where its gap is, and has a deliberate practice for managing it.

Name the gap honestly

The most damaging thing a program can do is insist the seam is not there. In my study, both students and faculty identified the same main concern long before anyone had language for it. Leaders who invite that conversation get usable data; leaders who treat it as a criticism get silence and a slow drift toward irrelevance. I would rather run a program that can articulate its own weak points than one that reports clean numbers it does not believe.

Design for the role, not the average

One of the clearest findings in my research was that role position determines what a person can actually do about a problem. Students responded to the gap by building self-directed study systems; faculty responded by adjusting pedagogy. Same concern, entirely different levers. Institutional interventions that ignore this — a single initiative aimed at everyone — reliably underperform. Good program leadership asks who holds which lever, and resources accordingly.

Make the improvement durable

I have led enough transitions to know that the real risk is not the change itself but what evaporates afterward. Documentation, methodology artifacts, standardized workflows, and honest handoffs are not administrative overhead — they are how an institution keeps what it learned. I build them into the work from the start, on the assumption that I will not be the one running the program forever.

Lead with evidence, decide with judgment

I spend a great deal of time making data legible: dashboards, KPIs, root cause analysis, outcome mapping. That work earns its keep only when it sharpens a decision somebody has to make. Metrics are an input to judgment, not a substitute for it, and a leader who cannot explain a number's limitations should not be acting on it.

Teaching and learning philosophy

I have taught soldiers, field technicians, project managers, and executives. What they had in common is what every adult learner has in common: existing expertise, limited time, and an immediate practical reason for being in the room. Andragogy is not a theoretical preference for me — it is the only thing that has ever worked with the people I teach.

Start from what the learner already carries

Adults arrive with a working model of the domain, and that model is the most valuable instructional asset available. My job is to find where it is accurate and build on it, and where it is misaligned and surface that productively. Instruction that ignores prior experience wastes the learner's strongest resource and quietly insults them.

Anchor every objective to a performance

I write learning outcomes as things a person can be observed doing, then map assessment directly to them. If I cannot describe what competent performance looks like, the objective is not ready. This is also the discipline that keeps the theory–practice gap from widening: outcomes tied to real practice are harder to drift away from.

Choose the modality the content deserves

Blended delivery is not a compromise between classroom and online — it is a design decision about which parts of the content need presence and which do not. Procedural knowledge often travels well asynchronously. Judgment, escalation, and anything with social stakes generally does not. I have designed both, and I have traveled to sites to run hands-on instruction when that was what the material actually required.

Build the artifacts that outlive the session

Quick-reference guides, knowledge base articles, standard operating procedures, facilitator notes. Training that exists only in the room ends when the room empties. The reusable support library is often the more valuable deliverable, and it is the one that accelerates every person who joins after you.

Treat assessment as feedback, not verdict

The point of learner assessment is to tell both of us what to do next. When results are disappointing, the first question is what the design got wrong — sequencing, practice opportunity, unclear expectations — before it is a question about the learner.
