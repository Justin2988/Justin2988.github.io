Enterprise Training & Adoption Program

Designing the instruction that made multi-site software deployments actually take — built on adult-learning principles, delivered where the work happens, and documented so it outlived the rollout.

Role

IT Project Administrator

Organization

Mears Group, Inc.

Period

August 2021 — February 2022

Scale

15+ IT professionals · 5+ major releases · Multi-site

01 Context

A regional operations technology environment was moving through a dense sequence of software releases — five or more major rollouts, with budgets between $150,000 and $500,000 and more than 12,000 combined resource hours. The technology side of a deployment is bounded and knowable. The human side is not.

Field staff across multiple sites were being asked to change how they did their jobs, often mid-season, often with no appetite for it. Deployments that ignore this produce a predictable signature: a spike in support tickets, workarounds that quietly defeat the system's purpose, and adoption numbers that look fine on paper while the old process survives underground.

02 My role

I led cross-functional IT project delivery for the environment, managing 15+ IT professionals across the release portfolio. Within that, I owned the design and delivery of the end-user training and onboarding programs that accompanied each rollout — treating enablement as part of the deployment rather than something bolted on after go-live.

03 Approach

  • Design from andragogy, not from the feature list. Training was built around what staff needed to be able to do, applying adult-learning and instructional-design principles rather than walking through software menus.
  • Layered materials for different moments of need. Full training programs for initial competency, quick-reference guides for the moment of use, and knowledge-base documentation for the question that arises three weeks later.
  • Hands-on facilitation at the site. Traveled onsite to deliver instruction in person during product rollouts, where the material required presence and where resistance is best addressed face to face.
  • Standardization across teams. Built documentation that made learning consistent from site to site, so a technician moving between locations encountered the same expectations and the same reference material.
  • Onboarding as a reusable asset. The same library that supported the rollout accelerated onboarding for new staff afterward — the training outlasted the project that created it.

04 Outcome

  • All initiatives across the release portfolio delivered on time and within budget — 12,000+ combined resource hours.
  • Built staff competency and drove technology adoption across sites.
  • Reduced post-deployment support demand — the clearest available signal that the instruction worked.
  • Standardized learning across teams and measurably accelerated onboarding.
  • Two promotions in four years, in recognition of performance, training leadership, and cross-functional collaboration.

Reduced support demand after a rollout is the honest measure of training. It is the one number that cannot be produced by a well-attended session nobody learned anything in.

05 Why this matters in higher education

This is faculty and staff development, with the vocabulary changed. A new LMS, a revised assessment platform, a change to advising workflow — each asks experienced professionals to alter established practice, and each fails the same way when enablement is treated as an afterthought.

The design discipline is identical: define the performance, build to it, meet learners where the work happens, and leave behind artifacts that keep working after the project team disbands.
